The problem & the thesis

Space debris and the limits of the liability regime

Low Earth orbit is a finite, shared resource carrying an accumulating population of derelict objects. This page sets out why that accumulation is a legal as well as a technical problem, and the argument developed in the underlying paper for pricing orbital risk rather than litigating it after the fact.

The externality

Each launch captures a private benefit while distributing a diffuse cost across the whole orbital environment: the collision risk its objects impose on every other operator. That cost is unpriced. Operators already bear it indirectly, through the propellant and downtime spent avoiding conjunctions, but nothing in the current framework charges the creation of the hazard itself. Absent a price, the individually rational choice is to keep adding objects, and the shared resource degrades.

Why the liability regime does not reach it

The 1972 Liability Convention imposes absolute liability for damage on the Earth's surface but only fault-based liability for damage in orbit, the category into which almost all debris collisions fall. Establishing fault requires a traceable causal chain from a specific object to a specific harm. The debris that most often disables a satellite is small, uncatalogued, and generated through successive earlier collisions; it cannot be traced to any launching state. The most probable cause of loss is therefore the least legally actionable, and the regime built for discrete, attributable incidents does not engage with a stochastic, cumulative hazard.

Modelled distribution of orbital risk by launching state

Aggregate Orbital Risk Score by country of origin across the on-orbit population. The large unattributed share is derelict material with no identifiable owner, the population a fault-based regime structurally cannot address.

The argument

The paper this work operationalises argues for a shift from ex-post attribution of blame to ex-ante quantification of risk. Rather than asking who caused a given collision, usually unanswerable, the framework asks how much risk each object imposes, prices that risk, and levies it as a contribution to a collective fund. Liability becomes a function of measurable orbital behaviour rather than of proving causation. The Orbital Risk Score is the metric that makes this operational; the compensation fund is the instrument that gives it financial effect.

The emphasis is preventive. Because the contribution tracks the score, and the score tracks choices an operator controls (disposal timing, passivation, manoeuvrability, orbit selection), the mechanism is designed first as a deterrent against launching high-risk objects, and only second as a source of compensation. The Methodology page sets out exactly how the score and the contribution are computed.
